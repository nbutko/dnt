M3 Scope: The Character Sheet

Abilities, levels, dice, weapons, items, and a Shop — the D&D layer that turns a typist into a hero.

M2 answered "what happens between battles?" — the map, dungeons, hearts, coins, XP. M3 answers "who is the kid playing?" It takes the parts of Dungeons & Dragons we haven't captured yet — a character with ability scores, a class, a level, dice rolls, weapons, and a bag of consumables — and wires each of them into the typing loop that already works, so that a battle stops being "type the line" and becomes "type the line as your character." The core loop from M2 (map → dungeon → branching encounters → boss) is not touched; everything here hangs off of it.

Stylized screen mocks for every surface below — character creation, the reworked Inn (rest, sheet, Armory), the Shop, the encounter d20, chest premonitions, item & weapon handling, and the in-battle flashes — live in the M3 wireframes. Several design calls captured in this doc (equip-at-Inn-only, the bag drawer, the signed CHA price modifier, HP-full-at-the-Inn) were pinned down while drawing those mocks.
This milestone deliberately supersedes the old Roadmap M3 ("power-ups & juice"). Power-ups survive here as consumable items, but the milestone's real job grew: it's the whole D&D character layer, since M4 is shipping and M5 is tuning — every mechanical element has to be in the game before the M5 balance pass can tune it. Screen-shake/SFX "juice" rides along where the dice and damage already create natural animation moments, but it is not the point of M3 and never blocks it.
Design north star for M3: when a real D&D rule and a typing-game need can be made the same thing, make them the same thing. Ability modifiers are the numbers in the damage/HP/time formulas; the attack roll is which passage you get; the damage die is the number that pops when you land a hit. The kid should be able to learn how his character works and, without noticing, be learning how D&D works.

The character sheet: six abilities

The heart of M3 is the standard six D&D ability scores. Each score's modifier — mod = floor((score − 10) / 2), exactly as in 5e — feeds directly into a combat number that already exists (see the damage/HP/time formulas). Every ability has one primary effect that carries its weight, plus a lighter flavor effect, so no stat is ever a dump stat but the sheet still stays legible to a 10-year-old.

These are the mappings that survived a "does the fantasy match the mechanic?" test. STR→damage, CON→HP, INT→"how hard a word can you handle," WIS→"how much time your composure buys you," CHA→"talk the shopkeeper down / rattle the monster" all read true. DEX is the one that carries two things on purpose — precision (crits) and nimbleness (dodging a hit) are both squarely DEX in D&D, and giving it a defensive angle keeps it from being a pure crit stat.
Modifiers, not raw scores, drive every formula — a score of 10–11 is a flat +0, so a fresh character with average stats plays exactly like today's untuned baseline, and every point of a modifier is a small, countable step (e.g. "+1 crit tier," "+one reading-buffer chunk"). Exact per-point magnitudes are a tuning knob, deferred to M5 with the rest of the numbers.

Leveling, the D&D way (this replaces the skill tree)

M2 spent XP in a bespoke five-branch skill tree. M3 throws that out and uses D&D's own progression, because it's both more authentic and, once abilities exist, simpler: XP is no longer a currency you spend — it's a total that carries you across level thresholds automatically, and levels hand out the same three things every D&D level does.

  • Automatic on level-up (non-discretionary):
    • Hit points — each level adds your class hit die + your CON modifier, scaled to dnt's existing HP magnitudes (a level-1 character sits near today's playerMaxHp = 40).
    • Proficiency bonus — the classic +2 / +3 / +4 / +5 / +6 ramp across the level range. It's added to every encounter d20, so simply leveling up makes your dice land in tougher-but-stronger prompt bands more often. This is the single number that means "you are just better at this now."
    • Class features — each class unlocks its signature perks at set levels (e.g. a second Bardic Inspiration die, a bigger Sneak Attack).
  • Discretionary (the choice moment): Ability Score Improvements. At levels 4, 8, 12, 16, and 19 — exactly the 5e cadence — the player gets +2 points to spend across the six abilities (or +1 to two of them). This is where the kid shapes his hero: pump STR to hit harder, INT to read harder words, CON to survive. It's the only thing he actively decides at level-up, which keeps the moment meaningful instead of a wall of choices.

XP itself still comes from the same sources M2 already pays out (per-kill, boss, real chest, scaled by dungeon tier). The 5e XP-to-level table (0, 300, 900, 2700, 6500, 14000, …) is the starting point; whether it wants compressing so the kid levels at a satisfying pace against dnt's actual reward economy is an M5 tuning question, not a scope one.

The Inn becomes a "rest," organized into tabs. With XP no longer spent on nodes, the Inn is split into Rest & Sheet (review stats, spend pending Ability Score Improvements, take a long rest) and Armory (equip/swap your weapon — see there). The skill-tree UI is retired; its five branches are absorbed into the ability system (Endurance→CON & the level HP/heart gains, Wordsmith→INT's tier cap, Focus→WIS, Luck→DEX & luck items, Utility→consumables). This also resolves the M5 "activate the deferred Focus/Luck/Utility branches" task — there are no branches to activate anymore.
What the long rest actually restores: hearts, not HP. HP already resets to full at the start of every battle (the M2 rule — HP is a per-battle pool; hearts are the per-run lives). So a character is always at full HP when standing in the Inn, and the "long rest" is really about recovering spent hearts (plus clearing any lingering state). Mid-run heart recovery without the trip back is the job of the Potion of Healing.

Classes

Four classes at launch, each a distinct answer to "how do you want to type?" A class sets your hit die, nudges your starting ability spread, hands you a starting weapon, and grants one signature feature that grows with level. More classes are content, not scope — the framework holds any number.

Wizardd6 (fragile)INT, WISWand (d6, arcane) Arcane Mind: your INT tier-cap sits one tier above normal, and your crits roll the damage dice three times instead of two. A glass cannon that types the hard words for huge hits.

Four is enough to make the choice feel real without a paradox-of-choice screen. They map cleanly onto typing temperaments: Fighter = "let me just get better slowly and not die," Wizard = "I want the big numbers," Rogue = "I want luck and crits," Bard = "I want an easier economy and a safety-net reroll." Each is anchored by its real 5e hit die and favored stats so the flavor is honest.

Character creation

Today a fresh save is just defaultSave(). In M3, when IndexedDB holds no valid save, the game opens a one-screen creation flow before the world map. Every step is pre-filled with a valid random default, so a kid who just wants to play can mash one button and get a complete, sensible character — but each piece is his to change:

  1. Roll your abilities. Six 4d6-drop-lowest rolls, shown as actual tumbling dice — the first and most literal "this is D&D" moment, and a natural teaching beat for what the numbers mean. A limited "roll again" re-rolls the whole set, so nobody starts the game stuck with a dud spread.
  2. Pick a class. Defaults to a random one of the four; the pick applies its hit die, favored-ability bonuses, starting weapon, and feature.
  3. Name your hero. Defaults to a random fantasy name from a bundled list; freely editable text.
Rolled stats (with a reroll), not point-buy. Point-buy is the "correct" balanced way and is exactly the kind of spreadsheet a 10-year-old should never see. Rolling is the fun, chaotic, table-authentic way; the reroll is the one guard rail so a genuinely unplayable spread never sticks. Any lingering imbalance gets smoothed by levels and gear anyway.

Dice: two rolls, neither interrupts typing

Dice are the D&D flavor we're missing most, but there's a hard constraint: while the kid is actually typing, he can't spare attention for anything. The HP bars are already near the limit of what he can track mid-line. So M3 uses two dice with opposite jobs, and neither ever asks him to stop typing to look at one.

1. The encounter d20 — rolled once, before you type

The moment you commit to a fight, the encounter reveal modal (the one built in M2 for mimics and chokepoints) rolls a single dramatic d20. This is the perfect home for it: it's a discrete beat before the clock starts, so it can be as big and animated as we like. The roll decides which of the dungeon's three prompt-difficulty bands you'll type this fight: 

total = d20 + proficiencyBonus + item/class bonuses     (e.g. a Luckstone, a Wand)

 natural 1  → FUMBLE:  low band, no crits, damage ×0.75 this fight  ("you stumble")
 total 2–7  → low band     (the easy end of this dungeon's text-tier range)
 total 8–13 → mid band
 total 14–19→ high band    (the hard end — a longer/nastier passage that hits harder)
 natural 20 → INSPIRED: high band, first landed hit is a guaranteed crit  ("a surge of focus")
  • Higher roll = harder passage that hits harder, which is why leveling (proficiency) and luck items matter: they push you up the bands. It stays fair because the band is always capped by your INT — you're never served a tier you literally can't read; if the dungeon out-tiers your INT you get served your cap and eat the existing squared tier-gate penalty (which INT now drives instead of the retired Wordsmith branch).
  • Natural die vs. total, exactly as in D&D: the band uses the modified total (so a Luckstone can shove a mediocre roll into the high band), but the fumble and the inspiration trigger only on a natural 1 or 20 — modifiers can't manufacture a crit.
  • The bands are read straight off each dungeon's textTierRange — the config field that exists today but nothing currently consumes. Low = bottom of the range, high = top.
This is also the fix for a real M2 wart: the player perceived prompt difficulty as randomly jumping around within a dungeon (because it was silently pinned to each monster's textTier). Turning that hidden randomness into a visible, named, buff-able d20 roll converts a "why did that get hard?" moment into a "come on, twenty!" moment — same variance, wildly better feel.

2. The weapon damage die — rolled every hit, automatically

In D&D, when you hit you roll your weapon's damage die (dagger d4, longsword d8, greataxe d12…). dnt does the same: each time you complete a prompt correctly, we roll your equipped weapon's die and fold it into the damage, then pop the result as a quick number over the monster. The kid never has to do anything with it — it's automatic juice — but it gives every weapon a felt personality (a d12 greataxe swings between feeble and devastating; a d4 dagger is steady) and turns "roll high" into a tiny thrill on every single hit. This is the answer to "can we roll dice on every attack?" — yes, as long as the roll is automatic and decorative, never a task.

The damage formula absorbs this by replacing the flat baseDamage with the weapon die + ability mod:

hitDamage = (roll(weapon.damageDie) + abilityMod) × damageScale
          × lengthFactor × speedBonus × critMult × powerUpMult × tierGatePenalty

  abilityMod   = STR (melee) / DEX (finesse) / INT (arcane), per the weapon
  damageScale  = constant chosen to keep magnitudes near today's baseDamage = 10
  critMult     = on a crit, roll the damage dice TWICE (D&D rule) — bigger AND swingier
  powerUpMult  = set by consumable buffs (the existing, currently-unused hook)
  lengthFactor, speedBonus, tierGatePenalty = unchanged from M2
Two dice, two clearly separated jobs: the d20 decides the shape of the fight (which passages, before typing), the weapon die decides the size of each blow (during typing, hands off). Crits live in both worlds — a natural-20 encounter roll guarantees one, and a per-hit crit chance (from DEX + the weapon's crit range) can fire on any blow — and both cash out the same exciting way: roll the damage dice twice. That double-duty is what makes "you rolled a 20" and "that hit critted" both feel like the same D&D jackpot.

How abilities resolve: the no-mid-typing rule

Each ability and class feature above is only a fantasy phrase until you pin down what happens, and when. One governing constraint does most of that work: a round is pure typing, so nothing may demand the player's attention while the clock is running. Every ability therefore resolves in one of three safe windows:

  • ① On the map / node-select — before you commit to a fight (sensing mimics, reading a chokepoint's threat).
  • ② In the encounter modal — the pre-typing beat that already hosts the d20 (rerolls, the intimidate readout).
  • ③ Passively, folded into a formula — automatic; reactive or defensive ones fire as a flash over the character, never a prompt.
The hard rule: any ability that sounds reactive or defensive — dodge, Second Wind, intimidate — is automatic plus a visual flash, because the kid cannot press a button mid-line. This isn't a compromise; it keeps those abilities as pleasant surprises ("Dodged!") rather than one more thing to track while racing a clock.

Sensing mimics

The richest of these earns its own rules, and it replaces the vague "spot the real chest." When you select a chest, a hidden check rolls d20 + WIS mod + proficiency (a Rogue adds Expertise — double proficiency) against that dungeon's mimic deception DC, which climbs by tier so deeper mimics hide better. Then:

  • Only a mimic can ever trigger it — a real chest never does. So a warning is a trustworthy "this one is a mimic!", while no warning stays ambiguous (a real chest, or a mimic you failed to sense). You can't back into the real chest just by never being warned.
  • On a success (a mimic, and you beat the DC): a modal — "Something about this chest sets your teeth on edge…" — offers Back away (skip it; the node stays available, no heart risk) or Open anyway (take the fight knowingly, for the XP). The knowledge is permanent for that chest.
  • On a failure, or any real chest: it simply opens — loot, or the mimic's ambush — exactly as it does today.
Probabilistic, not X-ray: a success reveals that this chest is a mimic, never which chest is the real one, so it rewards perception without dissolving the core gamble. Because the DC scales with tier, WIS stays worth raising all game — a stat you could out-level into irrelevance would be a dead stat.
Which rolls the player sees. The mimic check stays hidden — only a success ever surfaces (as the warning), preserving the tension of every un-warned chest. Combat rolls do the opposite: the encounter d20, crits, and dodges all flash, because seeing them is the fun. Default is no false alarms on a real chest — reliability builds a 10-year-old's trust in the mechanic — though a rare false positive is a spice dial we can turn on later if detection ever feels too safe.

Weapons: persistent gear

A weapon is the one piece of persistent equipment — unlike consumables, it stays with you across dungeons until you swap it. You equip one at a time. A weapon never adds an in-battle action (typing stays pure); it just reshapes the battle math passively via its die, its governing ability, and its crit range.

You equip only at the Inn's Armory tab, between dungeons — never mid-run. Consumables are prepped from the dungeon map because they're spur-of-the-moment; a weapon is a run-long commitment you live with, so swapping is locked once you enter a dungeon. This keeps gear choice meaningful (you can't hot-swap the perfect weapon per encounter) and keeps the dungeon UI free of an equip screen.

A STR-heavy Fighter wants the greataxe; a DEX Rogue wants the dagger's crit range; an INT Wizard wants the wand. The greataxe's "−time budget" is the one risk/reward knob — it makes WIS (time) matter more when you wield it — and is exactly the kind of number M5 will tune or cut.

Items: per-run consumable buffs

Consumables are the "power-ups" the old roadmap promised, shaped by a firm rule from the brief: items are never used during a round — a round is pure typing. You buy them at the Shop, they sit in your inventory (persisting across sessions like any owned thing), and you spend them from the dungeon map, before you pick your next fight. Their effect applies to the next fight (or the rest of the run), and all active item effects clear when the run ends — win, wipe, or leave. Unused items stay in the bag; only the buff is per-dungeon.

The bag is an expandable drawer, not an always-on panel. On the dungeon map it lives behind a 🎒 Bag button that opens a drawer over the graph and closes when you pick a node — because the branching path needs the screen room and prepping should be a deliberate beat, not permanent clutter. What does stay pinned is a small active-buffs strip showing which effects are riding on your next fight (e.g. "Bull's Strength · next fight," "Luckstone · rest of dungeon") and that they clear when you leave — so the player always knows what's in effect without opening anything.

Note several items buff the encounter d20 (Luckstone, Guidance) — that's the brief's "a classic buff is a modifier to dice rolls," made literal.

The Shop

The Shop has sat locked on the world map since M2 as a "Coming soon" placeholder. M3 opens it, and coins — earned and displayed all through M2 but with no sink whatsoever — finally have somewhere to go. It sells consumables and weapons; consumables restock each visit, weapons are one-off purchases. Your CHA modifier adjusts every price (and a Bard stacks its Silver Tongue on top). This is the coin economy's only sink, the mirror of the Inn being XP's (now-retired) sink.

The CHA price modifier is signed. A positive CHA charms the keeper into a discount; a negative CHA (the dump-stat Fighter with CHA 8, mod −1) marks prices up instead — a small, felt tax that makes CHA a real trade-off rather than a one-way freebie, and surfaces in the Shop as a visible banner ("Charisma −1 → prices up 5%") so the kid understands why gold buys less.

What treasure and monsters drop

M2 chests and kills paid out flat coins/XP. With gear and consumables now existing, the brief's "revise what chests/monsters drop" opens up — kept simple:

  • The real treasure chest can now drop a weapon, a consumable, or a coin hoard, instead of only coins/XP — making the "which chest is real?" gamble genuinely exciting again.
  • Mimics are unchanged — still a fight, still cost a heart on a loss; that tension is already fun.
  • Regular monsters keep dropping coins + XP (your Shop and leveling fuel). Bosses add a larger payout and can guarantee a gear drop, so a first clear feels like a real haul on top of the tier unlock.

Open questions

XP pacing. The real 5e XP table vs. a compressed one, and where the level cap sits relative to the 11 dungeon tiers — needs a play-pace pass in M5. Levels should feel frequent early and meaningful late.
Per-point magnitudes. Exactly how much a +1 modifier is worth in each formula (damage %, crit %, time-budget ms, HP, INT tier-cap, Shop discount %, dodge chance, intimidate wpm cut), the per-tier mimic deception DCs, the Second Wind threshold/heal and the Sneak Attack die, plus the damageScale that keeps the new weapon-die damage near today's feel, and the fumble/inspiration magnitudes. All M5 knobs; M3 ships sensible placeholders.
HP scale: keep dnt's magnitude, borrow D&D's structure, reject its low-level lethality. What sets how many prompts a battle lasts is hits-to-kill = monster HP ÷ player damage-per-hit — a ratio, independent of absolute scale. dnt already tunes that to a healthy multi-prompt band by scaling monster HP and player damage together (tutorial Gray Ooze 40 HP ≈ 3–4 prompts; the Grassland boss 150 ≈ ~10; and both sides climb in lockstep through the Tarrasque's 1447). D&D's low-level ratios don't give us that: a level-1 hero (~10 HP) and CR-⅛ fodder (a 7-HP goblin) one-shot each other — exactly the cheap feel we're avoiding, and worst right where a beginner starts (D&D only earns good resolution around level 5+). So we keep dnt's larger numbers — they're the right order of magnitude, already roughly tuned, and they give the per-hit weapon die and the speed/length multipliers smooth resolution instead of coin-flip swings — and we borrow D&D only for the relationships: hit-die size sets relative class toughness and the per-level HP step, the weapon die sets relative per-hit power and variance, mods/proficiency are the +Ns. The one arbitrary number — today's 40 — stops being magic: level-1 HP = a hit-die-derived value × a scale factor (~3–4), so a d10 Fighter lands near 40 and a d6 Wizard a bit under. The exact scale factor and per-tier hits-to-kill remain M5 knobs.
Weapon/item pricing & Shop restock. Prices, whether the Shop's stock is fixed or rotates per visit, and whether weapons can also be found (chests) as well as bought. Economy-tuning territory.
Does M3 want a light juice pass? The dice and damage numbers create obvious animation moments (a tumbling d20, a crit flash, a floating damage number). Deciding how much of the old roadmap's hit-flash / screen-shake / SFX list rides along now vs. waits is a scope-trim to make when the implementation plan is written.
