Combat Tuning Targets

What "beating a dungeon boss" should cost — in level, wpm, and battle time — before we touch a single knob.

This is step B.1 of the M4/M5 combat retune: write down the theoretical targets first, so the simulation pass (step A) has something concrete to measure against, and the change plan (step B.2) has a gap to close. Nothing here is implemented yet — every number is a target to be validated or adjusted by the sim, not a tuned value. Where a cell is a hard anchor (given directly, not interpolated) it is marked.

The retune exists because wiring the 14-tier content (content/text/library.json) into the M0-era combat math stretched the difficulty surface past what it was tuned for: prompts now run to ~2000 chars (was ~220), damage scales linearly-and-uncapped with prompt length (engine/damage.ts's lengthFactor), and the hits-to-kill balance invariant is currently parked (it.skip). See Game Design → Damage for the formula this doc is trying to re-anchor.

1. The three-corner model

Every dungeon is tuned to a character level and a typing speed. But a player rarely sits exactly on that mark, so each boss has a tradeoff surface: level + gear on one axis, typing wpm on the other. They substitute for each other. We name three points on it:

  • On-track — the anticipated level + kit, typing at the dungeon's expected speed. The center. Should win comfortably-but-not-trivially.
  • Ahead — a prodigy typist racing ahead of the level curve: under-leveled, weaker kit, but fast hands carry it. Winnable, but tense.
  • Behind — a grinder catching up: over-leveled, strong/maxed kit, slower hands. The level + gear buy back the speed they lack.
Two hard anchors given, everything else interpolated between them:
  • Dungeon 1 boss (T4): on-track = L2 @ ~10 wpm; behind = L4 @ ~7 wpm; ahead = L1, easy for any moderately competent typist.
  • Dungeon 11 boss (T14): on-track = L15 @ 100 wpm, high-but-not-maxed kit; behind = L20 @ 85 wpm, maxed kit; ahead = L12 (difficult), L10 basically impossible.

Two clean regularities fall out of those anchors and let us fill the ladder without guessing:

  • On-track level ≈ round(1.3·(N−1) + 2) — D1→L2, D11→L15, ~1.3 levels per dungeon.
  • Behind-corner wpm ≈ the previous dungeon's on-track wpm, and ahead-corner wpm ≈ the next dungeon's on-track wpm. (This is exactly the given "grind D11 at 85, which is dungeon-10's target." Over-leveling lets you fight a dungeon at the pace of the one below; racing ahead means fighting it at the pace of the one above.)

3. Battle shape — length, prompts, time

"Battle time" is the product of two things the fight is made of: how long one prompt takes (its char length ÷ the player's speed) and how many prompts it takes to win (monster HP ÷ damage per hit). The first is content; the second is what the retune actually tunes (via monster HP), so it is the free variable the sim will set to hit a target battle time.

(The ~15 min at T14 assumes a couple of reads/retries on a clean-ish run; three flawless 2000-char prompts alone are ~12 min.) This is the resolution of the earlier "single-passage vs. repeated-prompts" fork — both: keep the long passages, and make a boss several of them.

The corners aren't just "same fight, more or less of it" — they're two different ways to win, each leaning on different stats:

  • Ahead → burst / race. Under-leveled means thin HP, so you can't afford a long fight — every monster attack window is dangerous. You win by ending it fast: high INT pulls bigger, harder-hitting prompts, and fast hands land several of them in rapid succession, dropping the monster before your HP does. Fights run shorter than on-track. Leans on INT (heavy hits) + raw typing speed, with DEX dodges to shrug off the few hits that land.
  • Behind → attrition / outlast. Over-leveled but slower-typing, so the fight is genuinely longer — the same attacks, just at a slower cadence. You survive the long haul with CON to soak the extra incoming hits and WIS to pad the typing timer so slow hands still land inside the budget. You take longer, but you get there.

So battle time is not corner-invariant: ahead compresses it, behind stretches it. The sim tunes monster HP so both archetypes — and on-track between them — converge on a win from opposite directions.

These are builds (how you fight), a different axis from the ahead/on-track/behind corners (where you sit on the level/wpm curve) — though they have affinities. Three coherent builds fall out of the existing abilities:
  • Glass cannon — INT + DEX + speed. Read up, hit hard, dodge, burst. Natural fit for the ahead corner.
  • Tank — CON + WIS. Soak hits, pad the timer, outlast. Natural fit for the behind corner.
  • Brute / sandbagger — STR + CON, low INT. Deliberately roll into the dungeon's easy prompts and crush them with raw weapon damage and flawless, reliable execution. The forgiving, low-variance path (see §5).
That all six abilities anchor a build is evidence the knob set is right; whether the magnitudes make each viable for every class is the §5 question.

Regular monsters vs. bosses — and why last dungeon's boss is this dungeon's warm-up

Bosses are the walls; regular monsters are the runway. Two rules keep progression feeling powerful:

  • Regulars die fast — ~2–3 prompts at the dungeon's on-track level and speed. They are where you rehearse the tier, not where you grind.
  • Bosses are the epic spikes — the #prompts / battle-time above, climbing to a 15-minute final-boss marathon.

The satisfying part is the overlap. Each tier of content appears twice: as dungeon N's boss (T = N+3) and as dungeon N+1's high-band regular (dungeon N+1 serves [N+1, N+3]). A regular carries far less HP than a boss, and you've grown a level besides, so content that was an epic boss becomes a routine regular one dungeon later. Concretely: T13 is Dungeon 10's 4-prompt epic boss; by the start of Dungeon 11 a T13 high-band regular should fall in ~2 prompts — and, typing at the same speed, feel comfortable rather than white-knuckle. Then T14's final boss towers over it. "What wrecked me last week is now my warm-up" is the power fantasy we're tuning toward.

A subtlety worth pinning down: the sharpened target is about pressure, not raw speed. Gaining a level should primarily make a given tier feel less frenetic — more HP and hearts to absorb mistakes, and (via WIS, or a small automatic per-level bump) more headroom on the typing timer, so you can take the current tier at a leisurely pace instead of racing the clock. Fewer prompts can follow when ASIs raise damage, but that's secondary; the felt reward is margin. The corollary is what keeps the ladder climbable: that same margin makes the next tier approachable — proficiency nudges you into it, and your new HP/timer let you survive its mistakes. So sweepLevelingMonotonicity should assert "a level never makes a tier feel tighter" (HP-remaining and miss-tolerance trend up), not merely "win-rate never drops."

4. Within a dungeon: the five-stage ramp

A boss is the capstone of a run, not a standalone. The dungeon graph already ramps difficulty across five checkpoints — entrance → early fights (first branch) → waypoint → late fights (second branch) → approach → boss — carried by the monster roster (weak early, tough at the approach) and, at the boss, the shifted text-tier window. The per-stage target is a smooth interpolation: a run's first fights sit near the previous dungeon's boss difficulty, and the ramp climbs to this dungeon's boss row above. Full per-stage targets are out of scope for B.1 (bosses first); they get their own table once the boss numbers survive the sim.

5. Mechanical prerequisites these targets assume

The tables above are only reachable if three structural things change first — they are assumptions baked into the targets, flagged here so the sim tests them explicitly:

INT gates the curriculum, and D&D can't hand out enough of it. Today intTierCap hard-caps the served tier, and leveling gives at most +8 INT across 20 levels — so an under-INT hero is walled out of level-appropriate content, then squared-penalized by tierGatePenalty on top. Fix: make INT a modifier on the encounter d20 (alongside proficiency) instead of a cap. The full [N, N+2] window is always reachable; INT merely nudges toward the high end. The squared gate penalty retires — but is replaced, not deleted: a mild tier reward (higher served tier → more damage per hit, up to the lengthFactor cap) so reading up is worth something without ever punishing reading down. That positive pull is also what keeps sandbagging honest (see the brute prerequisite below). Boss window shifts from {N+3} to [N+1, N+3] (grassland boss rolls 2–4, nudged toward 4). Content for this already ships.
Fights must not collapse to a single exchange. Uncapped lengthFactor means one well-typed high-tier hit can exceed a boss's whole HP bar (and a crit doubles it), so the "#prompts" column above is currently unachievable at high tiers — the fight ends in one hit. Fix: author monster HP in "reference-hits" units so hits-to-kill is banded by construction, and soft-cap lengthFactor so no single hit/crit exceeds a sane fraction of HP. Then re-enable the parked invariant against this table's #prompts targets. This is doubly load-bearing now that stamina bosses are deliberately multi-passage (§3: 6 / 5 / 4 / 3 prompts): if one well-typed 2000-char hit can end the fight, a 15-minute three-passage T14 boss is impossible to build. The HP + lengthFactor cap is exactly what lets a boss survive long enough to demand all its passages.
Every class needs a viable path to each row, and the gear ladder is too short. A whole crit subsystem (DEX crit chance, weapon critRange, the crit-boost item) is computed but ignored by damage.ts — so the Rogue's identity is missing and Oil of Sharpness is a no-op. And weapons stop at shop-tier 3 (top die d12): a player buys their last weapon before dungeon 4, so "high-but-not-maxed vs maxed kit" — which the D11 anchors depend on — doesn't exist past the early game. Fix: wire crit; extend the weapon/gear ladder the length of the game (per governing ability, so each class upgrades), and add persistent defense/HP gear so survivability is a real progression axis. Items should be able to counter-balance a character-sheet flaw at each price point.
Support the third build: the brute / sandbagger. A low-INT, high-STR player can lean the other way — deliberately roll into the dungeon's easy prompts and win on raw weapon damage plus flawless, reliable execution (short prompts = near-max speed bonus, almost no misses or timeouts, so every hit lands near its potential). For this to be a genuine tradeoff and not a dominant exploit, higher tiers must pay more per hit (the tier reward in the INT prerequisite above) — the brute forgoes that reward in exchange for consistency. Two properties make it safe for a trainer: it is self-limiting (sandbagging caps you at the dungeon floor tier N, which still rises every dungeon, so the curriculum keeps climbing — it just spares the ceiling N+2), and it is an accessibility valve (a struggling kid can fight even a boss at N+1 instead of N+3 — the 15-minute T14 wall at T12 passages: still winnable, less glorious). Enabler needed: proficiency always pushes the roll up, so the brute needs an item to pull it down — cleanest is a disadvantage trinket (roll 2d20, keep the lower), robust at every proficiency level; optionally a brute stone (−N to the roll, +STR-damage). Keep it to one item — this is a one-kid game, not a build-crafting MMO.

6. What the simulation pass (step A) must answer

engine/sim/balance.ts's simulateCharacterBattles already models the whole pipeline — class, level, abilities, weapon, buffs, encounter roll, served tier, real computeDamage — and reports win rate, hits-to-kill, crit rate, dodge rate. The sweep should print, for every (dungeon boss × corner × class):

  • Win rate at the target (level, wpm) — on-track should be a comfortable win, ahead a marginal one, behind a safe one, and the "impossible" cells (e.g. D11 @ L10) should actually lose.
  • Hits-to-kill / #prompts vs. the §3 targets — separately for regulars (target ~2–3) and bosses (the 4→6→…→3 epic curve). The gap tells us how far monster HP (and the lengthFactor cap) has to move.
  • Level-up eases pressure — at a fixed tier and wpm, a higher level should leave more HP remaining and more miss-tolerance (calmer, not just winnable), and open the next tier. Track HP-fraction-at-win and timer headroom, not only win-rate. (The "T13 boss → T13 warm-up" prompt drop, ~4 → ~2, is mostly the regular's smaller HP bar, not leveling — keep the two effects separate.)
  • Class spread — the four classes should all clear each row via some gear path; the spread between best and worst class at a fixed (level, wpm) is the balance error to close.
  • Build viability & sandbag calibration — glass-cannon, tank, and brute should all be able to clear on-track; and critically, sandbagging (roll-low + high STR) must be a tradeoff, not strictly dominant. Sweep tier-reward strength against the lengthFactor cap until reading up and playing the floor are within a reasonable band of each other.
  • Sensitivity — how many wpm one character level is "worth," and vice-versa, so the ahead/behind corners are the right distance from center.

The output of step A is a measured surface laid next to this target surface. Wherever they diverge is a line item in the step-B.2 change plan (knob values, HP authoring, new gear, crit wiring).
