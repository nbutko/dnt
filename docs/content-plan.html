Content Plan

What he actually types, and what he's fighting.

Difficulty tiers

Typing content is organized into tiers. The skill tree's "Wordsmith" branch unlocks access to later tiers (longer/harder text); early monsters only ever draw from early tiers. This is also roughly a typing curriculum, borrowed from how sites like typing.com / Keyboarding Without Tears sequence lessons.

7Multi-sentence passage (2–4 sentences)The knight raised her sword. The dragon didn't even blink. Something told her this fight would take a while.

9Multi-paragraph passage2–3 linked paragraphs
10Short story excerpta full scene or chapter-length excerpt
Content should be genuinely fun to read, not filler pangrams — jokes, silly monster trash-talk, game-y flavor text. A 10-year-old will type "The goblin says your shoes are untied" far more willingly than "the quick brown fox." Keep a running bank of these and add to it like adding new levels.

Where the text comes from

Tiers 1–6, for now: a small hand-authored corpus

  • Hand-authored banks for launch — a few hundred lines per tier is plenty to start and keeps quality/tone controlled (this is the recommended path for M1–M3; see Roadmap).
  • Store banks as simple JSON/TS arrays keyed by tier, so adding content later is a data change, not a code change.
  • Optional later idea: let him submit his own favorite lines (jokes, Minecraft trivia, whatever he's into) into a custom bank — high buy-in, near-zero engineering cost.

Tiers 7–10, later: public-domain children's literature

Once the game reaches the long-form tiers, hand-authoring paragraphs stops being the efficient move — but templated/generated text also isn't necessary. Classic public-domain children's books (Peter Pan, Alice in Wonderland, The Secret Garden, Tom Sawyer, etc., e.g. via Project Gutenberg) already provide a huge body of genuinely well-written, varied long-form text, free to use with no licensing concerns. Chop them into paragraph/scene-length excerpts per tier and there's essentially unlimited tier 7–10 content without writing or generating a word of it.

Resolved: no templated/auto-generated text at any tier. Short tiers (1–6) stay a small curated, game-themed bank written by hand; long tiers (7–10) scale via excerpted public-domain books instead of generation. Both are "real" writing, which matters more for a reading-and-typing tool than infinite variety would.
Not needed for the near term (see Roadmap M6 — tiers 7–10 are part of the later visual/long-form content pass, not the core M0–M5 build), but worth deciding later: pick excerpt boundaries by hand for readability/pacing, or split mechanically (e.g. every N sentences)? Hand-picking is likely worth the one-time effort for tier 10 in particular, where a short story needs to actually make sense as a battle unfolds across it.

Monster roster

Monster stats are just data (JSON), so the roster grows without new code. Stats match the simulation in Monster typing: wpm (speed), accuracy (chance per character it's typed wrong), and attention (mean seconds before it notices a mistake — not a per-character roll, since noticing is time-based). Starting sketch:

As of M2, "tier" stops meaning a single flat 1–4 difficulty scale and starts meaning dungeon tier — one of 11, each tied to a real-world-manual habitat with its own CR/WPM/text-tier band. See the M2 scope doc for the full 11-tier table. The roster below becomes the concrete Tier 1 (Grassland) content: Slime, Goblin, and Skeleton as regular fights, Slime King as the tier's boss. Wraith and Dragon don't fit Grassland's CR band and are reserved as placeholders, to be reassigned to an appropriate tier during the M5 content pass rather than kept on a flat scale.

Difficulty ramps mostly through monster HP and text tier, not absurd monster WPM — the point is to make the player type more/better, not to make the race unwinnable. Early monsters pair a low accuracy with a slow (high-second) attention, so they visibly flub and dawdle over fixing it; later monsters ramp both wpm and attention together, since a fast monster with slow attention would spew a huge correction every time it errs (attention is measured in seconds, so more wpm alone already means more wrong characters typed per mistake — see Monster typing).

Visual identity

Start fully text-based — a text-adventure-style presentation, no images at all. The battle "screen" is just text: a description of the monster, its HP as a bar or number, its live typing rendered character by character (typos and corrections included, per Monster typing), the player's own prompt/input box, and a simple on-screen keyboard that highlights the next few keys as a touch-typing aid (see the M0 plan). Hits and misses can still read as punchy through text alone (color flash, a shake via CSS transform, a damage number popping up) without a single drawn asset.

No image assets until a later, separate visual-upgrade pass (see Roadmap M6). Art is the single most time-consuming, hardest-to-scope part of a game like this — deferring it means the entire core loop (M1–M5) can be built and playtested on nothing but layout and typography, and images get added on top of a proven game rather than gating it.
